SEO Audit — Knight Group Handyman Services
knightgroup.com  ·  Safety Harbor / Pinellas County, FL  ·  Full site audit
Prepared July 1, 2026
Executive summary
Knight Group&apos;s site has a genuinely strong technical and content foundation for a business of its size: clean URL structure, per-service and per-city landing pages (over 30 location/service combinations), free written estimates and transparent pricing spelled out on-page, and consistent NAP (name, address, phone) across Yelp, Facebook, and Nextdoor. That is more programmatic SEO infrastructure than most local handyman competitors bother building.
Three things are holding that foundation back. First, live searches for the site&apos;s own core terms — “handyman Safety Harbor FL” and “handyman Clearwater FL” — do not surface knightgroup.com in the top organic results at all; national franchise Mr. Handyman and directory listings (Yelp, Angi, Thumbtack) currently own that real estate. Second, Google&apos;s indexed titles and snippets for the homepage and several service pages still reflect the site&apos;s old identity as an “Auto Services &amp; Remote IT Solutions” business — a rebrand that happened in 2024 but hasn&apos;t fully propagated through Google&apos;s index, which actively hurts click-through on a stale, confusing snippet. Third, there is a real, verifiable text-encoding bug (mojibake) corrupting em dashes and punctuation in meta descriptions and visible FAQ copy across multiple pages — this is not a cosmetic nitpick, it will show as garbled characters directly in Google search snippets and on the live page.
Overall assessment: needs work, but from a good base. The quick wins below (fixing the encoding bug, requesting re-indexing on stale titles, tightening duplicate meta descriptions) are low-effort and should be done this week. The strategic priority is building topical authority and review volume to actually rank the location pages that already exist — right now Knight Group has the pages but not the visibility.

Content gap recommendations
Trust and differentiation content
Topic: “Why a plumber-background handyman matters” (owner Vince Knight&apos;s 15 years as a journeyman plumber). Why it matters: this is Knight Group&apos;s single strongest differentiator versus every other local handyman on the search results page, and it&apos;s currently buried in About/Plumbing copy rather than given its own page. Format: long-form trust/authority article. Priority: high. Effort: moderate (half day).
Topic: “Licensed vs. unlicensed handyman work in Florida” — what a handyman can legally do vs. when a licensed plumber/electrician is required. Why it matters: high informational search volume, builds E-E-A-T, and is exactly the kind of clear factual Q&amp;A content that AI answer engines (ChatGPT, Perplexity, Google AI Overviews) prefer to cite. Format: guide/FAQ page. Priority: high. Effort: moderate.
Topic: rental turnover / punch-list repairs for landlords and property managers. Why it matters: Vince Knight&apos;s 10 years in Florida property management is mentioned but there&apos;s no landing page targeting that specific, higher-frequency-buyer audience. Format: dedicated landing page. Priority: medium. Effort: moderate.
Commercial / conversion content
Topic: “No 2-hour minimum” as a standalone landing page, not just a pricing-page bullet. Why it matters: this is a concrete, competitor-beating differentiator (the pricing page&apos;s own franchise comparison shows $150 vs. $340+) that deserves to rank on its own rather than live inside the pricing page. Format: landing page. Priority: high. Effort: quick win (1–2 hours).
Topic: seasonal hurricane-prep and storm-damage handyman content. Why it matters: Pinellas County is hurricane-exposed, search demand spikes seasonally, and Knight Group already does storm-related repair work (boarding, weatherproofing per the emergency-services and pricing pages) but has no dedicated seasonal content to capture that spike. Format: seasonal blog/landing page, refreshed annually. Priority: medium. Effort: moderate.
Structural gaps
No blog or regularly-updated content section was found anywhere in the crawled site — all content is static service/location pages. A lightweight blog (project write-ups, seasonal tips, pricing explainers) would give Google and AI crawlers fresh content signals and more long-tail entry points. Priority: medium. Effort: substantial (ongoing).
Only 7 Google reviews are referenced site-wide. Content and outreach that drives review volume (a formal post-job review-request flow) is a content-adjacent gap: competitor trust signals (directory presence, review count) currently outweigh Knight Group&apos;s on-site proof. Priority: high. Effort: moderate (process change, not content per se).

GEO / AI-visibility check (llms.txt, AI crawler access)
No llms.txt file found at /llms.txt or /ai.txt. Neither is strictly required for AI visibility today, but an llms.txt is a low-cost way to hand ChatGPT, Claude, and Perplexity a clean, structured summary of services, service areas, and pricing rather than relying on them to parse the full site.
robots.txt does not block any AI crawlers (no rules targeting GPTBot, ClaudeBot, PerplexityBot, Google-Extended, etc. were found), so nothing is actively preventing AI models from crawling and citing the site — but the malformed robots.txt (missing User-agent line, noted above) should be fixed so that intent is unambiguous to every crawler, human-authored or AI.
The site&apos;s heavy use of plain-text FAQ blocks (nearly every page ends with a Q&amp;A section) is exactly the content shape AI answer engines prefer to lift and cite. This is a genuine existing strength — the recommendation is to formalize it with FAQPage schema (see Technical checklist) and to build the two informational pages recommended in Content Gaps (“licensed vs. unlicensed handyman,” pricing FAQ) specifically because they answer discrete, citable questions.
The direct, crawlable link to the Google Business Profile (via the maps.google.com/maps?cid= link on the homepage and About page) is a positive GEO signal — both traditional local search and AI systems increasingly lean on GBP-linked data for local recommendations.
Slow/hanging responses on non-existent paths (see Technical checklist) are worth fixing partly for this reason: AI crawlers commonly probe for /llms.txt as a first step, and a hung response instead of a fast 404 is a worse experience for that crawler than a clean “not found.”
